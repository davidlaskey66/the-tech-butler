The Smart Host's Guide to Short-Term Rental Technology

Published March 16, 2026 • 6 min read

Running a short-term rental in the Collingwood and Blue Mountains area has become significantly more competitive over the past few years. Properties that were booked solid simply by existing now compete with professionally managed listings that offer seamless check-in, reliable Wi-Fi, and five-star guest experiences from the moment someone arrives.

Technology is not the whole picture — but it is a meaningful part of it. The right setup reduces the time you spend managing guest issues, protects your property, and contributes to the kind of reviews that drive bookings. Here is what experienced hosts in this market are using.

Smart Locks: The Foundation of Self-Check-In

Keyless entry is the single most impactful technology upgrade for a short-term rental. It eliminates key handoffs, lost key situations, and the need to coordinate your schedule with guest arrival times. Guests receive a unique code before check-in, enter on their own schedule, and the code expires automatically at checkout.

The management benefits go beyond convenience. Every entry is logged with a timestamp, so you know exactly when guests arrived and departed. Cleaning crew codes can be enabled for specific time windows after checkout. If a guest reports a problem on arrival, you can confirm remotely whether they actually entered the property.

For properties in Blue Mountains and Collingwood where turnover can be rapid during peak ski season or summer weekends, the time savings of not coordinating key exchanges adds up significantly over a busy season.

Noise Monitoring

Noise complaints from neighbours are one of the most common problems short-term rental hosts face — and one of the most damaging to a property's standing on booking platforms. Noise monitoring devices measure decibel levels in common areas without recording audio or video, which means they are privacy-compliant while still giving you visibility into whether a gathering is getting out of hand.

When noise levels exceed a threshold you set — typically late at night — you receive an alert and can contact guests before the situation escalates to a neighbour complaint. Devices like Minut and NoiseAware are purpose-built for this use case and are widely used by professional hosts.

Reliable Wi-Fi

As covered in a previous post, Wi-Fi complaints are the most common guest issue in short-term rentals. A mesh Wi-Fi system with a dedicated guest network is the standard for well-run properties. Guests get fast, reliable connectivity in every room. You get a separate network for your management devices — cameras, locks, thermostat — that is not affected by guest usage.

Change the guest network password between stays as a standard practice. Most mesh systems make this a 30-second task from a phone app.

Smart Thermostat

A smart thermostat serves two purposes at a rental property. For guests, it provides comfortable temperature control. For you, it provides remote monitoring and the ability to set limits — preventing guests from running heating or cooling at extreme settings that drive up energy costs.

Between stays, the thermostat automatically returns to a property-maintenance temperature rather than continuing to heat or cool an empty property. Over a season, this adds up to meaningful energy savings.

Outdoor Security Cameras

Exterior cameras covering the driveway and entry points provide visibility into guest arrivals and departures, monitor for unauthorized visitors, and serve as a deterrent against property misuse. Cameras must be disclosed to guests — this is a platform requirement on Airbnb and VRBO — and should never be placed inside the property.

For properties that have experienced damage or unauthorized parties in the past, cameras are a practical deterrent that also provides documentation if a damage claim needs to be filed.

Guest Instructions and Digital Guidebooks

Technology only works if guests know how to use it. A well-organized digital guidebook — delivered via a link in your check-in message — covers Wi-Fi credentials, lock codes, TV and streaming instructions, thermostat operation, and any other systems in the property.

Keep instructions brief and visual where possible. Guests do not read long paragraphs on their phones after a four-hour drive. Step-by-step instructions with clear headings, or short video walkthroughs for complex systems, reduce the messages you receive asking how things work.

Building the Right System

The most common mistake hosts make is adding technology piece by piece without a plan, ending up with devices from multiple ecosystems that require separate apps and do not work together. A smart lock from one brand, a thermostat from another, and cameras from a third can all function individually — but managing them separately is more work than the original manual approach.

Starting with a platform decision — typically Google Home or Amazon Alexa for rental properties — and selecting devices that integrate within that ecosystem produces a system that can be managed from a single app, with automations that coordinate across devices.
