5 Signs Your Smart Home Needs a Professional Tune-Up

Published April 1, 2026 • 5 min read

Smart home systems are not set-and-forget. Devices go offline. Firmware updates change how things behave. New devices get added without being properly integrated. Routines that worked perfectly for a year quietly stop working after an app update.

Most homeowners tolerate these issues gradually — restarting a device here, ignoring a broken automation there — until the system is significantly less useful than it was when first installed. A professional tune-up resets everything to working order and often improves on the original setup based on how the home is actually being used.

Here are five signs it is time to have someone look at your system.

1. You Have More Than One App for Things That Should Work Together

A smart home that requires four separate apps to manage is not a smart home — it is a collection of independent devices. If you are opening a Ring app for your doorbell, a separate Nest app for your thermostat, a Kasa app for your plugs, and an Arlo app for your cameras, nothing is actually working as a unified system.

A proper tune-up consolidates device management into a single platform wherever possible, sets up the integrations between devices, and creates the routines that make disparate devices behave as one coordinated system. You should be able to manage everything that matters from one app and a few voice commands.

2. Devices Drop Offline Regularly

A device that goes offline once a year is normal. A device that goes offline weekly — or that you have to restart regularly to keep functioning — is a sign of an underlying problem. The most common cause is Wi-Fi signal quality at the device location. The second most common cause is an overloaded router struggling to maintain connections to too many devices.

A mesh Wi-Fi assessment and node repositioning often resolves persistent offline issues without replacing any devices. In some cases, a device that appears to be failing is simply operating at the edge of its Wi-Fi range and will work reliably once coverage improves.

3. Your Routines Have Stopped Working

Automation routines are the part of a smart home system most likely to break silently. An app update changes how a device reports its status. A platform update changes which trigger conditions are supported. A device is replaced with a newer model that uses a different integration.

If your Good Morning routine no longer adjusts the thermostat, or your Goodnight routine stopped locking the door, the issue is usually a broken link in the automation chain that takes a few minutes to diagnose and fix — once you know where to look.

4. You Have New Devices That Are Not Connected to Anything

This is extremely common. A new smart bulb gets installed in the bedroom but never added to the existing lighting group. A new security camera gets set up in its own app but never integrated with the main platform. A new smart plug gets connected to Wi-Fi but never assigned to a routine.

Each unintegrated device is a missed opportunity — and often a source of the fragmentation described in sign one. A tune-up brings everything into the existing ecosystem, ensures devices are properly grouped, and updates routines to include new hardware.

5. You Do Not Know If Everything Is Still Working

If you cannot confidently say whether your outdoor camera is online right now, or whether your smart lock's battery is charged, or whether your smoke detector is still connected to the app — your system needs attention.

A properly configured smart home is one where you have visibility into the status of every device. This is not about constant monitoring — it is about knowing that if something stops working, you will be notified immediately rather than discovering it during an emergency or a guest complaint.

What a Professional Tune-Up Includes

A smart home tune-up is not a sales visit. It is a systematic review of what you have, how it is configured, and what needs to change to make everything work the way you intended.

  • Device audit — every device checked for connectivity, battery level, and firmware status
  • Wi-Fi assessment — signal strength verified at each device location
  • Platform consolidation — devices moved into a single ecosystem where possible
  • Routine review and repair — broken automations diagnosed and fixed
  • New device integration — recently added devices properly connected and grouped
  • Walkthrough — you understand what is working, what was fixed, and how to manage it going forward
