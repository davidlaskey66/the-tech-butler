Smart Home Technology for New Homeowners: Where to Start

Published March 24, 2026 • 6 min read

Moving into a new home is one of the best opportunities to set up smart home technology properly. You have a blank slate — no legacy devices to work around, no existing ecosystem to accommodate, no bad wiring decisions baked into the walls.

It is also a moment when it is easy to overbuy. The excitement of a new home combined with aggressive smart home marketing leads a lot of new homeowners to purchase devices that sit in boxes, get returned, or create a fragmented system that is more frustrating than useful.

This guide covers how to think about smart home technology as a new homeowner — what to prioritize, what to hold off on, and how to build a foundation that grows well.

Start with the Network

Before purchasing any smart home devices, assess your Wi-Fi coverage. Walk through every room and check signal strength. Note where the coverage is weak — typically bedrooms furthest from the router, garages, and basements.

If your home is larger than 1,500 square feet, has multiple floors, or has rooms with weak signal, invest in a mesh Wi-Fi system before anything else. Every smart home device you add will depend on reliable Wi-Fi. Starting with a solid network prevents the most common source of smart home frustration.

Choose One Platform and Stick With It

Decide early whether you are building around Google Home, Amazon Alexa, or Apple HomeKit. This decision shapes which devices you buy, which voice assistant you use, and how well everything works together.

The right choice depends on what you already use. Android phone users and Google service users should choose Google Home. Amazon Prime users with Echo devices should choose Alexa. Apple device users should consider HomeKit. If you are starting fresh, Google Home and Amazon Alexa are equally capable — choose based on which feels more natural to use.

Once you have chosen a platform, buy devices that are natively compatible with it. Avoid devices that require a separate hub or a workaround integration — they work less reliably and create more maintenance over time.

The First Three Upgrades Worth Making

1. Smart thermostat

This is the highest-value first upgrade for most homeowners. It pays for itself in energy savings, provides remote temperature control, and integrates with every major smart home platform. Install it first and get comfortable with the app before adding other devices. Recommended: Google Nest Learning Thermostat, Ecobee SmartThermostat.

2. Smart lock and video doorbell

Keyless entry and the ability to see who is at the door from anywhere are genuinely useful daily. A smart lock eliminates the risk of lost keys, allows you to let in contractors or family members remotely, and integrates with your automation routines. A video doorbell provides awareness and security without requiring a full camera system.

3. Smart lighting in high-use areas

Start with one or two rooms rather than the whole house. The living room and primary bedroom are the most impactful starting points. Smart switches are a better long-term investment than smart bulbs — they work with any bulb and do not require the physical switch to remain on.

What to Hold Off On

Not every smart home product is worth buying immediately. These categories are better approached after you have lived in the home for a season and understand how you actually use the space:

  • Smart blinds: High cost, installation complexity, and battery maintenance. Worth it in the right rooms, but not a priority early.
  • Full security camera systems: Start with a doorbell and one outdoor camera, then expand once you know which angles and locations actually matter.
  • Smart appliances: Smart fridges, ovens, and washing machines add cost and complexity for features most people rarely use.
  • Multi-room audio: A worthwhile upgrade, but better approached as a deliberate project than an impulse purchase.

Think About How the Home Will Be Used

Smart home priorities differ significantly based on how you will use the property. A primary residence in Collingwood has different needs than a vacation property in Blue Mountains, and both differ from a property being used as a short-term rental.

For a primary residence, comfort and convenience are the priority — thermostat, lighting, and voice control make daily life easier. For a vacation property, security and remote monitoring matter most — cameras, smart lock, and network reliability. For a short-term rental, add noise monitoring and guest management tools.

Getting Set Up Right the First Time

The most common regret I hear from homeowners is not starting earlier — it is starting without a plan and ending up with a fragmented system that needs to be rebuilt. A short consultation before you start purchasing saves significantly more time and money than sorting out compatibility problems after the fact.
