How to Prepare Your Vacation Property for Spring

Published March 12, 2026 • 6 min read

After a Georgian Bay winter, vacation properties need attention before the spring and summer season begins. Pipes, roofing, and landscaping get most of the focus — but the technology systems in your property deserve the same seasonal review.

A camera that was knocked offline by a winter storm, a smart lock with a depleted battery, or a Wi-Fi router that reset itself months ago can leave your property unprotected and unmonitored without you knowing. Here is a practical technology checklist for vacation property owners heading into the spring season.

Wi-Fi and Network

Start here, because everything else depends on it. Log into your router's app and confirm all nodes are online and showing normal status. Check that your internet service is active and that speeds are reasonable — ISPs occasionally throttle or change plans on properties that show low usage over winter months.

If you experienced power outages over the winter, your router may have reset to factory settings, which means your network name and password have reverted to defaults. Any smart device that was connected to your network will be offline until reconnected. This is the most common tech problem discovered at the start of the spring season.

Confirm your guest network is set up with the credentials you want for the upcoming season, and update the password if you share it with short-term rental guests.

Smart Locks

Check battery levels on all smart locks — most apps report this directly. Battery-powered locks that sat through a cold winter will often be significantly depleted, even if they were fully charged in the fall. Replace batteries proactively rather than waiting for a low-battery warning during a guest stay.

Test every access code that should be active — your own, any caretaker codes, and any codes left from previous guest stays that should have been deleted. Confirm the lock connects to the app and responds to remote commands.

If the lock behaved erratically over winter — slow to respond, failing to report status — cold weather may have affected the internal components. Some smart locks perform significantly better after a firmware update, which the app will prompt if one is available.

Security Cameras

Open your camera app and confirm every camera is online and showing a live feed. Check the field of view on each camera — winter ice, fallen branches, or snow accumulation on mounts can shift camera angles significantly. Adjust as needed so coverage is back to where it should be.

Review any motion alerts from the winter period. Cameras that detected activity while the property was supposed to be empty are worth reviewing — most will be weather-related false alerts, but it is worth confirming.

Clean camera lenses. Condensation, dust, and spider webs accumulate over a closed winter season and reduce image clarity noticeably.

Video Doorbell

If your doorbell is battery-powered, check charge level and recharge or replace as needed. Test the two-way audio — speakers and microphones can be affected by moisture over winter. Confirm motion alerts are active and set to the sensitivity level you want for the busy spring and summer season.

Smart Thermostat

Update your thermostat schedule for the spring and summer season. The setback temperatures appropriate for a vacant winter property — typically 10-12°C to prevent pipe freezing — are not appropriate for a property being actively used or rented. Set comfortable occupied temperatures and appropriate away temperatures for the warmer months.

If your property uses both heating and cooling, confirm the system switches correctly between modes as outdoor temperatures change. Some thermostats require a manual mode change; others switch automatically based on a target temperature range.

Smoke and CO Detectors

Test every detector. Smart detectors will report their status in the app, but a physical test confirms the alarm itself is working. Replace batteries in any detector showing low battery status. Check the manufacture date — smoke detectors should be replaced every ten years, CO detectors every five to seven years.

Streaming Devices and TVs

Streaming devices that have been unplugged or powered off for months often require software updates before they work properly. Power everything on and allow updates to complete before guests arrive — this can take 15-30 minutes per device and is worth doing in advance rather than during a guest check-in.

Confirm streaming service logins are still active. Subscription changes or password updates on your primary accounts can log out devices at the property without warning.

For owners who cannot visit their property at the start of the season, a professional technology check-in covers all of the above — verifying every device is online, testing every system, updating firmware, replacing batteries, and confirming remote access is working correctly before your first guests of the season arrive.
